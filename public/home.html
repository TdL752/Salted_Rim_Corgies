Why Everyone Falls for the Pembroke

The Pembroke Welsh Corgi is one of the world's most beloved herding breeds — bold, bright, and bursting with personality in a surprisingly compact package.

Size & Build

Corgis typically weigh 22–31 lbs and stand 10–12 inches tall. Their long body and short legs are their trademark — built low to the ground for agile herding.

Intelligence

Ranked among the top 15 most intelligent dog breeds, Corgis are quick learners who thrive on mental stimulation. They love a challenge — and will outsmart you if you're not careful!

Temperament

Loyal, playful, and surprisingly bold for their size. Corgis are affectionate with family, friendly with strangers, and gentle with children when properly socialized.

Energy Level

Moderately active — they love a good run or hike but are equally happy snuggling on the couch. Two 30-minute walks a day keeps a Corgi happy and healthy.

Coat & Colors

The classic double coat comes in red, sable, fawn, black & tan, and tri-color, always with white markings. They shed seasonally — a good brush twice a week works wonders.

Lifespan

With proper care, Pembroke Welsh Corgis typically live 12–15 years, making them a long, wonderful commitment full of adventures and belly rubs.

Caring for Your Corgi

Everything you need to give your new best friend a happy, healthy life — from puppyhood through their golden years.

🍗 Quality Kibble First

Choose a high-protein, breed-appropriate kibble as the foundation. Look for real meat (chicken, beef, or salmon) as the first ingredient.

📐 Portion Control

Adult Corgis do best with ¾–1 cup of dry food twice daily. Avoid free-feeding — this breed loves food and obesity stresses their long spine.

🥦 Healthy Treats

Carrots, blueberries, and plain cooked chicken make excellent low-calorie treats for training. Avoid grapes, raisins, onions, and xylitol.

💧 Fresh Water Always

Keep clean water available at all times, especially after exercise. Stainless steel bowls are easiest to keep bacteria-free.

🐶 Puppy-Specific Needs

Puppies need 3–4 small meals a day until 6 months, then transition to twice daily. Use puppy-formulated food for the first year.

🚫 Foods to Avoid

Never feed chocolate, macadamia nuts, avocado, cooked bones, alcohol, or anything with xylitol — all are toxic to dogs.

🦶 Daily Walks

Two 30-minute walks per day is the sweet spot for adult Corgis — enough to burn energy without overworking their joints.

🎾 Playtime Counts

Fetch, tug-of-war, and interactive toys all contribute to their daily exercise. Mental stimulation counts as much as physical activity.

🧗 Stairs & Jumping

Limit repetitive jumping and use ramps for furniture access. Their long spine is vulnerable to disc disease if stressed repeatedly.

🐕 Herding Instinct

Corgis love having a job. Try agility, rally obedience, or even herding trials — activities that tap their natural drive keep them fulfilled.

🌡️ Weather Awareness

Avoid pavement walks on hot days — their belly is close to the ground. In winter, dry their paws after walks and watch for salt irritation.

🐾 Puppy Exercise Limits

For puppies under 1 year: 5 minutes of exercise per month of age, twice a day. Over-exercising puppies can damage developing joints.

🖌️ Regular Brushing

Brush 2–3 times per week with a slicker brush and undercoat rake. During spring and fall shedding season, daily brushing prevents tumbleweeds.

🛁 Bath Time

Bathe every 6–8 weeks using a gentle dog shampoo. Bathing too frequently strips natural oils — unless they've found something delightful to roll in.

✂️ Nail Trims

Trim nails every 3–4 weeks. Long nails cause discomfort and affect their gait. Start handling paws early so they tolerate it happily.

👂 Ear Cleaning

Check ears weekly for redness or odor. Use a vet-approved ear cleaner to gently wipe — never insert anything into the ear canal.

🦷 Dental Care

Brush teeth 2–3 times per week with dog-safe toothpaste. Dental chews and water additives help too — dental disease is very common in dogs.

✂️ Professional Grooming

A professional groom every 8–12 weeks keeps everything tidy. Note: never shave a Corgi's double coat — it protects them from both heat and cold.

🩺 Vet Check-ups

Annual wellness exams are essential. Puppies need several vet visits in their first year for vaccines, deworming, and early health screenings.

💉 Core Vaccines

Distemper, parvovirus, adenovirus, and rabies are non-negotiable. Your vet may also recommend bordetella and leptospirosis based on lifestyle.

🦴 Spine Health

Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD) is a known risk in Corgis. Maintain healthy weight, use ramps, and limit jumping to protect their back.

👁️ Eye & Hip Screening

Reputable breeders test for Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA) and hip dysplasia. Ask for health clearances from both parents before purchasing.

🐛 Parasite Prevention

Year-round flea, tick, and heartworm prevention is strongly recommended. Talk to your vet about the best options for your region and lifestyle.

✂️ Spay & Neuter

Most vets recommend waiting until 12–18 months before spaying or neutering Corgis to allow proper hormonal development and growth.

🎓 Start Early

Begin basic commands (sit, stay, come, leave it) at 8 weeks. Corgis are eager learners — early training shapes a well-mannered adult dog.

✨ Positive Reinforcement

Reward-based training works best. Use small, high-value treats and enthusiastic praise. Corgis respond poorly to harsh corrections — they'll dig their heels in!

🏠 Crate Training

A crate is a safe den, not a punishment. Introduce it gradually with treats and meals inside. Most Corgis learn to love their crate as a cozy retreat.

👥 Socialization

Expose puppies to new people, dogs, sounds, and environments between 8–16 weeks. Well-socialized Corgis are confident and friendly throughout life.

🐾 Leash Manners

Teach loose-leash walking early. Corgis can pull! A front-clip harness helps redirect their attention without putting pressure on their throat.

📚 Keep It Fun

Short sessions (5–10 minutes) work best. End on a success and always leave them wanting more. Bored Corgis will find their own entertainment — usually at your expense.
